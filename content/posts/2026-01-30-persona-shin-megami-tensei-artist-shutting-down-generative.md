The game from world-renowned artist of the Shin Megami Tensei and Persona series, Kazuma Kaneko, has revealed that his latest game, Tsukuyomi: The Divine Hunter, will be shutting down on April 22. However, Kaneko also announced that the game will be hitting the Nintendo Switch as a full-priced product.

The rogue-like deck-building game is riddled with generative artificial intelligence images, which, according to the company's claims, are trained on the artist's work. This is by design, as an "AI Kaneko", which is intended to generate new cards based on what the player has done in the game.

Rather than focus on building a quality deck builder, like the many, many, many, many similar games like it, the developers COLOPL went with quantity. As such, when the game hits Switch as an offline title, it'll only pull a rough 3,600 of the cards from the pile that were generated.

On Steam, the game sits with a Mixed overall review score, with players complaining about the artwork in the game. The top review states that the "precious few characters... that are actual real illustrations from Kaneko are all pretty good.

"But the moment you start seeing the AI demons... it completely breaks the illusion. The style isn't even slightly consistent, and they don't look like something Kaneko would have ever made."

Another pointed out that the game is actually pretty fun, while another pointed out that the further the generated cards got, the more it seemed like they were just rehashes of already generated ideas.

Generative AI game resurfacing as more reject tech

Generative AI is rapidly being rejected by the wider games industry on the surface. While under the hood, it's obvious some developers are using it to assist with coding, as Valve has moved to change its AI policy in the last week to state that it's what the player would directly interact with, being necessary for an AI disclosure, not that tools have been used.
