Congressional hearings can be dull affairs. But a gathering of the House Energy and Commerce committee on January 13, focusing on bills affecting the auto industry, shot off as many sparks as a dragging muffler.

At issue, among other proposed legislation, was a bill that would, for the first time, impose a federal safety-led framework on the Wild West of autonomous vehicles. In the absence of such legislation, regulation of self-driving has largely been left up to the states, which have taken a variety of approaches.

Have you wondered why so many self-driving programs are in California, Texas, Arizona, and (only occasionally) Michigan? These are the states that permit fully driver-free commercial operations and robotaxis with few restrictions. States that are putting up barriers include Connecticut, New York, Massachusetts, New Mexico and Pennsylvania.

Requiring any self-driving vehicles on public roads to be certified as safe under a federal program is an obviously laudable goal, but some critics say the Safely Ensuring Lives Future Deployment and Research in Vehicle Evolution Act of 2026 (Self Drive Act) discussed at that hearing isn't the way to go about it. Specifically, they say it would steam roll over any existing state regulation of autonomous vehicles, and let companies devise their own "safety case," which would then by reviewed "upon request" by the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA).

The Self Drive Act was introduced by members of Congress Bob Latta (R-OH) and Debbie Dingell (D-MI). The latter said at the hearing that she's a "car girl, born and raised in Michigan, the center of the American auto industry," and that her goal is promoting safety and helping the industry "stay competitive in a global marketplace."
